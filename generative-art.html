Contour figures

Drawing something that feels alive and has a human touch to it – it feels natural to do by hand (or by foot).

But the expressiveness and richness of detail that we associate with artistic craft, could it be simulated in generative art – and by what means could computer images speak in a non-obvious way, with aesthetic and emotional appeal? Can there be authentically erotic, or humorous, or touching, generative art, and with what cost?

These may seem rhetoric or blatant questions, but I think they need a good answer, especially because art and design are being ever more digitalized. And while some things become easier with the computer, some earlierly obvious things may even be forgotten altogether.

The Venus of Sine (variation ACU), 2016/09.

This is first one in a series of hominids I crafted when this idea of emotional figurative aspect in generative art started to intrigue me. Its body is shaped by two overlayed sine functions and its head is modeled separately. So it is "purely" generative.

Sense of the environment

One thing that comes naturally to the eye is recognizing relatively uniform arrays of things. In cave paintings figures stand side by side, distinct from each other, possessing their own space on the wall. This uniform randomness is surprisingly difficult to generate and maintain.

In nature, it represents itself in many things formed gradually by immense energy: the waves, the wind, the rocks, grains of sand, the rhythm of the trees in a forest.

Once again, my own intuitive reaction to recurring and rhytmic processes seems the "key" part here. What I find rhytmically stimulating is probably that for a good reason. Not unlike music, generative art creates an astonishing possibility to explore rhytmical interactions and the overlapping of different rhythms.
